Finetuning methods used in this project — which weights to update, how to shape data, and how to evaluate beyond train loss.

The Explorer shows the forward pass these methods train. This page covers the surrounding decisions: frozen vision vs. action expert, normalization, and post-training options (GRPO/DPO as reference only).

1 · The thing you're finetuning

A modern VLA is three parts wired in sequence. A vision encoder turns camera frames into patch tokens, a transformer backbone (usually a pretrained VLM) fuses those with the language instruction and the robot's proprioceptive state, and an action head reads it all out as motor commands.

   camera ----+
   prompt ----+---->  [ transformer backbone ]  ---->  [ action head ]  ---->  actions
   state  ----+         a pretrained VLM                flow matching          a(t .. t+H)
              |              (frozen?)                  or chunking
         patch tokens

"Finetuning" is the act of nudging some subset of those weights toward your robot's embodiment and your task — starting from a checkpoint that already knows how images, language, and actions relate, instead of learning it from scratch.

2 · Which weights to move: full finetune vs LoRA

The first decision is how much of the network to train. Updating everything fits your data best but is expensive and easy to overfit on small robot datasets. LoRA freezes the base weights and learns a tiny low-rank update ΔW = A·B inside the attention and MLP blocks — typically well under 1% of the parameters — which is cheaper, less destructive to the pretrained prior, and easy to swap per-task.

   FULL  FINETUNE                          LoRA   (parameter-efficient)
   +------------------------------+        +------------------------------+
   |  update ALL weights          |        |  freeze base W               |
   |  billions of params          |        |  train  W := W + A·B  (rank r)|
   |  best fit, most compute,      |        |  ~0.1-1% of params trainable |
   |  can wash out the prior       |        |  cheap, swappable, prior-safe|
   +------------------------------+        +------------------------------+

A common middle path — and the one I lean on for the flow-matching models — is to freeze the vision encoder, keep the backbone mostly fixed, and train the action expert (plus optionally LoRA adapters). The visual features generalize; what you mostly need to relearn is how to turn them into your robot's actions.

3 · The action head, briefly

How the head emits actions shapes everything downstream. Two families show up in this lab:

  • Action Chunking Transformer (ACT) — predicts a short chunk of future actions directly, trained with a CVAE objective. Simple, strong on tight manipulation.
  • Flow-matching expert (π₀ / π₀.5) — generates an action chunk by integrating an ODE from Gaussian noise to a clean action, conditioned on the tokens. This is the part the Explorer animates.
   tau = 0                                         tau = 1
   noise  ~~~~~~~~>  integrate  dX/dtau = v(X, tau)  ~~~~~~~~>  action chunk
   N(0, I)          a few Euler steps along the field          a(t .. t+H)

4 · Shaping the data

Robot learning lives or dies on the schema. The policy only sees what you put in the dataset, so the observation and action spaces have to be consistent and well-normalized.

  • Observation — the camera image(s), the language instruction, and a proprioceptive state vector (joint angles for an arm; pose + velocity for a vehicle).
  • Action — a chunk of future commands at a fixed rate. For the SO-101 that's 6-DoF joint targets + gripper; for the driving stack it's ego-frame motion deltas at 10 Hz.
  • Normalization — compute real per-dimension statistics from your data and normalize actions and state. Mismatched or missing norm stats are a silent killer; a policy trained on unnormalized actions can look like it's "not learning" when the data is the bug.

5 · Past imitation: post-training

Behavior cloning gets you a competent base policy, but it only ever imitates the demonstrations. To push past the demonstrator you post-train the BC checkpoint with a reward or a preference signal.

   demos ---> [ behavior cloning ] ---> base policy  pi_BC
                                            |
                +---------------------------+---------------------------+
                |                                                       |
         [ GRPO  ·  RL ]                                       [ DPO  ·  preference ]
         reward  +  KL-to-BC                                   chosen  >  rejected

GRPO — reinforcement fine-tuning

Sample several trajectories from the current policy, score them with a reward, and push probability toward the better-than-average ones. Updates use group-relative advantages (group mean as baseline), a KL penalty back to the frozen BC policy, and a PPO-style clip — with a learning rate gentler than the one used for pretraining.

   obs --> pi_theta --> sample K trajectories --> reward r
            ^                                          |
            |                                          v
            +----  KL anchor to pi_BC  +  clip  <----  advantage = r - mean(r)

DPO — learning from preferences

If you have pairs where one trajectory is preferred over another, DPO skips the reward model and directly raises the policy's score for the chosen trajectory relative to the rejected one, with a frozen BC reference keeping it from drifting. For diffusion/flow policies the preference strength β has to be large enough to actually saturate on a small flow margin — set it too low and the objective just keeps inflating the rejected trajectory's loss.

   ( chosen  >  rejected )  pairs
        |
        v
   raise   log pi(chosen) - log pi(rejected)        [ frozen pi_BC reference anchors it ]

6 · A practical recipe

The defaults I reach for when finetuning a flow-matching VLA — conceptual, not a config dump:

  • Freeze the vision encoder; train the action expert (± LoRA) rather than the whole stack.
  • Cosine learning-rate schedule with a short warmup; for post-training, drop the LR by an order of magnitude versus pretraining.
  • Hold out a validation split and watch open-loop action error (ADE), not just train loss.
  • Compute and apply real action/state normalization statistics from your own data.
  • For RL/preference tuning, watch the flow loss: bounded and flat means stable, climbing means it's diverging — re-anchor or lower the LR.
